## Evaluation rule

No candidate is preferred merely because it is common, easy to demonstrate, or described as a sandbox. The evaluation asks whether the complete composition can enforce, observe, and prove every mandatory property while treating the validator as hostile arbitrary native code.

The comparison distinguishes:

| Concept | Meaning in this package | What it does not prove |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Architecture class | The arrangement of trusted owners, enforcement layers, boundaries, transports, lifecycle, and acceptance gates. | Selection or correct configuration of an exact primitive. |
| Implementation mechanism | A concrete kernel, service, runtime, or hypervisor feature used to realize part of the architecture. | That the other required layers exist or compose correctly. |
| Packaging mechanism | A way to assemble or distribute software and artifacts, such as a container image. | Confinement, immutable executed-byte identity, or host isolation. |
| Deployment mechanism | A way to schedule, host, update, or operate attempts, such as a service, container platform, or VM fleet. | Generation-safe process ownership, denial policy, cleanup, or evidence completeness. |
| Security evidence | Externally observed, attempt-bound proof that exact controls enforced the required behavior on an exact platform profile. | Universal security, correctness on another profile, or future drift resistance. |

## Candidate set

All entries are **CANDIDATE** mechanisms or compositions.

| Candidate | Architecture characterization | Current disposition |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Direct OS-native primitives under a dedicated launcher | External launcher composes kernel process identity, resource, namespace, identity, handle, syscall, access-control, staging, transport, and cleanup controls directly. | **RECOMMENDED baseline architecture class**, subject to durable ownership and all OPEN mechanism decisions. |
| Direct primitives managed by a service manager | Dedicated supervisor delegates durable process-tree and resource ownership to a reviewed service manager while retaining attempt policy, staging, transport, evidence, and acceptance logic. | **RECOMMENDED ownership variant** when exact service behavior is independently reproduced. |
| Rootless container runtime | Unprivileged runtime and user mapping package and launch attempts with runtime-managed namespaces and resources. | Viable component, not a complete boundary and not automatically lower risk. |
| Rootful container runtime | Privileged runtime or daemon creates containerized attempts and kernel policy. | Viable component with a larger and more privileged trusted computing base. |
| Supervisor plus container runtime | External trusted supervisor controls attempt identity, artifacts, transports, evidence, and acceptance while a runtime supplies selected isolation and packaging functions. | Viable layered variant; runtime evidence must be subordinate to external acceptance. |
| Lightweight VM or microVM | Per-attempt or pooled small VM boundary adds a guest kernel and virtualized devices. | Higher-isolation or fallback candidate; not automatically required or sufficient. |
| Full VM | Conventional VM provides a guest OS boundary and operational separation. | Higher-isolation or fallback candidate with larger lifecycle and patching burden. |
| Hybrid VM host plus OS-native guest controls | VM separates the host while a guest supervisor applies the full OS-native composition inside the guest. | Strongest layered candidate in this set, with the largest trusted computing base and evidence burden. |

## Aggregate admission and device-interface handling

Every candidate must provide externally enforceable and observable aggregate admission and host-resource protection, not merely per-attempt limits. It must also expose no device or kernel-control interface by default and establish a minimal reviewed per-attempt inventory covering character and block devices, device-node creation, inherited device descriptors, `/dev`, `/proc`, `/sys`, cgroupfs, debugfs, tracefs, securityfs, applicable runtime or service control filesystems, and other applicable kernel-control interfaces. An inability to establish, observe, or preserve either boundary makes the exact profile unsupported.

| Candidate | Aggregate admission and host-resource protection | Device and kernel-interface handling |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Direct OS-native primitives under a dedicated launcher | Requires an external atomic reservation ledger plus host-level controllers, queues, counters, and recovery reserve; the launcher must deterministically refuse overcommit and reconstruct active reservations after restart. | Can construct a minimal mount, device, capability, descriptor, syscall, and mandatory-access-control policy directly; every allowed object and pseudo-filesystem must be inventoried and remount, namespace, path, and inherited-descriptor bypass tested. |
| Direct primitives managed by a service manager | Service or cgroup capacity can contribute, but a separate externally observed admission ledger must cover concurrency, non-controller resources, queues, audit buffers, cleanup backlog, and supervisor reserve across services. | Service mount and device restrictions can contribute only if the exact effective `/dev` and pseudo-filesystem views, inherited descriptors, kernel interfaces, and mutation denials are captured outside validator control. |
| Rootless container runtime | Delegated controllers may be incomplete; an external supervisor must prove enforceable aggregate ceilings and preserve host and recovery reserve rather than infer safety from individually valid container limits. | Rootless defaults and namespace labels do not establish the device boundary; runtime-created devices, mounts, helpers, inherited descriptors, and host pseudo-filesystem exposure require an exact external inventory and negative evidence. |
| Rootful container runtime | Broad controller authority can support aggregate quotas, but the privileged daemon, runtime queues, image and log storage, audit flow, retries, and cleanup backlog must be reserved and observed by an external admission authority. | Powerful device and mount configuration increases consequence of error; privileged devices, runtime sockets, host `/proc` or `/sys`, cgroupfs, tracing and security filesystems, device creation, and alternate mounts must be denied and externally evidenced. |
| Supervisor plus container runtime | The supervisor can atomically reserve capacity before runtime creation and correlate host, runtime, transport, storage, audit, retry, and cleanup consumption, retaining reservations through uncertain runtime cleanup or restart. | The supervisor can reject unsafe runtime device and mount specifications and compare requested runtime state with kernel-observed mounts, device objects, descriptors, capabilities, syscall policy, and mandatory-access-control state. |
| Lightweight VM or microVM | VM admission can reserve host CPU, memory, devices, monitor processes, descriptors, disks, transports, logs, and recovery capacity, while guest controls still govern aggregate guest-local resources when a guest hosts concurrent attempts. | A minimal virtual-device model can reduce host exposure, but monitor devices, guest `/dev` and pseudo-filesystems, guest kernel controls, host-guest channels, and inherited host descriptors require exact inventory and evidence at both layers. |
| Full VM | Fleet or host admission must reserve VM and management-plane capacity, storage, snapshots, logging, audit, recovery, and any guest concurrency; VM sizing alone does not bound aggregate guest work or operational backlog. | Virtual devices and guest kernel interfaces remain part of the attack surface; management agents, shared devices and mounts, guest pseudo-filesystems, kernel controls, and host integration channels must be minimized and independently inventoried. |
| Hybrid VM host plus OS-native guest controls | Host admission must reserve VM and monitor resources while guest admission reserves per-attempt capacity; linked evidence must prevent double counting, gaps, or premature release across host and guest restart and cleanup. | The VM layer minimizes host devices and interfaces while the guest applies the full OS-native device, mount, descriptor, syscall, capability, and policy boundary; inventories and denial evidence must be linked across both layers. |

## Recommendation

**RECOMMENDED:** use direct OS-native Linux confinement primitives under a dedicated external supervisor as the baseline architecture class, with a service manager, cgroup, job, VM owner, or reviewed equivalent responsible for durable generation-safe process-tree ownership. Compose separate enforcement for artifact provenance, filesystem exposure, private scratch storage, per-attempt resource budgets, aggregate admission and concurrent-host-resource governance, the minimal reviewed device and kernel-interface boundary, identity and non-escalation, inherited handles, ordinary network denial, local IPC isolation, syscall exposure, mandatory access control, dump policy, termination, cleanup, and evidence-gated result acceptance.

A service manager is a strong candidate for durable ownership, but no exact service implementation is selected. A container runtime is optional and may be used for packaging or operations; it cannot become the sole proof of security. Rootless execution, namespaces, seccomp, a low-privilege account, and AppArmor are each partial mechanisms rather than complete boundaries. A VM is not automatically required or sufficient, but lightweight VM, full VM, and hybrid host-and-guest profiles remain higher-isolation or fallback candidates if the baseline profile cannot meet the accepted requirements or if later risk decisions justify their additional trusted computing base and operational cost.

The full composition must be independently reconstructed and reproduced before platform support can be claimed. Exact mechanisms remain subject to P42-D004, P42-D005, P42-D006, P42-D007, P42-D008, P42-D009, P42-D010, P42-D011, P42-D012, P42-D013, P42-D014, P42-D015, P42-D016, P42-D017, P42-D018, P42-D019, P42-D020, P42-D021, and P42-D022.

P42-D003 cannot be owner-approved until P42-D001 is approved, the recommendation is reconciled against the exact selected platform profile, the reconciled package receives independent review, and the project owner explicitly approves P42-D003. D003-GAP-AGG-001 and D003-GAP-DEVICE-001 are already resolved only by their approved immutable traceability records. Approval of P42-D002, and any later approval of P42-D001, does not by itself approve P42-D003.

## Nonclaims

- No candidate is **APPROVED** by this analysis.
- No mechanism is implemented, tested, deployed, certified, or production-ready.
- Container packaging, rootless execution, namespace creation, syscall filtering, mandatory access control, low privilege, or virtualization alone does not satisfy P42-D002.
- JavaScript-level controls remain defense in depth and are not the Phase 4.2 OS security boundary.
- A mechanism available on an unapproved or development profile does not establish support on the eventual production profile.
- CI output or vendor documentation cannot prove that the project configured or composed a candidate correctly.
- Phase 4.2 remains **PLANNED**, and implementation remains prohibited while Phase 4.1 is rejected.
