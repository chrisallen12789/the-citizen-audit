The Citizen Standard

A free society does not only require accountable government. It requires citizens willing to handle evidence responsibly.

What a citizen owes the record

  1. Read before repeating. A claim should not travel farther than the evidence that supports it.
  2. Distinguish fact from inference. What a source says, what it implies, and what we conclude from it are not the same thing.
  3. Respect uncertainty. Unknowns are not permission to invent certainty.
  4. Apply the same standard to allies and opponents. Evidence does not become weaker because it harms a favored side or stronger because it harms an opposed side.
  5. Revise when the evidence changes. Changing position because the record improved is not weakness.
  6. Challenge with substance. A serious challenge identifies the claim, evidence, error, replacement evidence, and proposed correction.
  7. Preserve inconvenient records. Records that complicate a preferred narrative are often the records most worth keeping.
  8. Do not confuse visibility with proof. A popular post, viral clip, or repeated slogan is not automatically evidence.
  9. Disagree honestly. Reasonable people may read the same public record and reach different conclusions. The duty is to keep the disagreement informed.
  10. Protect the correction process. A public correction improves the record. It should not be treated as humiliation.

The habit this institution asks of readers

Before asking whether a claim confirms a belief, ask whether the claim can be traced. Before asking whether a finding is useful, ask whether it is bounded. Before repeating a conclusion, ask what would change your mind.

Civic principle

A citizen does not owe loyalty to a narrative. A citizen owes discipline to the record.
