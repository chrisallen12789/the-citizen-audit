First Reader Introduction

I Thought the Hard Part Would Be Finding the Numbers.

I was wrong.

For months, I set out to answer what I thought was a simple question.

Where does our money actually go?

I expected disagreements. I expected politics. What I did not expect was how difficult it would be to answer basic questions using public records.

The information was not always hidden. It was scattered.

One report measured one thing. Another measured something slightly different. One agency counted money one way. Another counted it another way. Some documents moved. Some were replaced. Some disappeared altogether.

More than once, I found myself spending hours just trying to determine whether two official government reports were talking about the same dollars.

That changed how I think about transparency.

We often say government information is public. But information is not truly public if ordinary citizens cannot realistically find it, verify it, and understand how it fits together.

Imagine taking a thousand-piece puzzle, throwing half the pieces into different rooms, replacing a few every year, and then telling people they have everything they need because all the pieces technically exist.

That is what researching public spending often feels like.

The biggest thing I learned was not about a single agency or a particular program.

It was this:

Transparency is not just publishing information.

Transparency means people can actually follow the evidence.

That means preserving documents. Showing your sources. Explaining your reasoning. Admitting what you do not know. And making it possible for someone who disagrees with you to check your work for themselves.

That is the standard I have tried to build toward.

I do not expect anyone to simply take my word for anything. In fact, I hope they do not.

If I have done my job correctly, you should not have to trust me. You should be able to check the evidence yourself.

This is just the beginning.
